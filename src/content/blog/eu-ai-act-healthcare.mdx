On March 23, 2026, the European Parliament voted 569-45 to support postponing key provisions of the EU AI Act through the Digital Omnibus package. For healthcare AI, this likely pushes high-risk system enforcement from August 2026 to **December 2027 for Annex III systems**, or **August 2028 for AI embedded in medical devices** regulated under MDR/IVDR. A dedicated medical AI carve-out is still under discussion in trilogue.

That sounds like good news. It is not.

The delay is not a reprieve from compliance -- it is a reflection of systemic unreadiness. Harmonized standards under CEN-CENELEC JTC 21 are still in draft. The European Commission has only designated a handful of Notified Bodies competent to certify high-risk AI. The interaction between the AI Act, the Medical Devices Regulation, and the General Product Safety Regulation remains unsettled. The Commission itself acknowledged in its February 2026 working document that the original timetable was "operationally infeasible."

Healthcare organizations that interpret this delay as permission to slow down will arrive at the new deadline with the same evidence gaps -- only now against a higher bar.

## What Actually Changed

The Parliament's March vote supports the Commission's Digital Omnibus proposal, which bundles several adjustments:

- **Annex III high-risk systems** (including most clinical decision support, triage, and risk prediction) shift from August 2, 2026 to approximately **December 31, 2027**.
- **AI systems that are safety components of MDR/IVDR-regulated medical devices** are expected to align with the device's own conformity assessment cycle, functionally pushing compliance to **August 2, 2028** for most manufacturers.
- A potential **medical AI carve-out** is under negotiation, which would subject clinical AI primarily to MDR/IVDR plus a reduced set of AI Act obligations, rather than full dual compliance.
- Prohibitions under Article 5 (February 2, 2025) and general-purpose AI model obligations (August 2, 2025) remain in effect. These were not delayed.

The delay is political and operational, not philosophical. The substantive obligations -- data governance, transparency, human oversight, post-market monitoring -- are not being relaxed. If anything, the additional consultation time is allowing Notified Bodies to raise documentation expectations.

## Healthcare AI Is Still High-Risk

Under **Annex III, Section 5**, AI systems used in healthcare-adjacent contexts remain classified as high-risk. This includes diagnostic and clinical decision support, triage algorithms, risk prediction models that influence coverage, and AI embedded in medical devices. The classification threshold did not move.

What did move is the requirement perimeter. Draft harmonized standards now circulating from JTC 21 suggest that acceptable evidence for Article 10 data governance will require granular subgroup performance reporting, not just aggregate metrics. Acceptable evidence for Article 15 robustness will require documented drift monitoring plans, not just one-time validation.

## Four Obligations That Still Define the Regime

### Article 10(2)(f-h): Data Governance and Bias Examination

The most specific bias-related obligations sit in Article 10, paragraphs 2(f) through 2(h). Training, validation, and testing datasets must be examined for possible biases, those biases must be corrected where possible, and relevant data gaps must be documented.

For healthcare AI, this means:

- Demographic representativeness assessed and documented across training, validation, and real-world deployment populations
- Bias detection applied throughout the lifecycle, not only at a single validation milestone
- Data collection methods, known gaps, and mitigation actions captured in the technical file

Draft JTC 21 guidance indicates that aggregate AUROC or accuracy statistics alone will not satisfy Article 10 for high-risk clinical systems. Subgroup performance across protected characteristics will be the baseline expectation.

### Article 13: Transparency and Instructions for Use

High-risk AI systems must be designed so deployers can interpret outputs and use them appropriately. For healthcare, this requires documentation of intended purpose, known limitations, and subgroup performance that clinicians and procurement teams can actually read. Generic model cards are unlikely to satisfy a Notified Body.

### Article 14: Human Oversight

Humans overseeing the system must be able to understand its capabilities, interpret outputs accounting for known biases, override recommendations, and halt operation. Interface design that encourages automation bias is a compliance risk, not just a usability issue.

### Article 72: Post-Market Monitoring

Post-market monitoring is governed by **Article 72**, not Article 61. Article 72 requires providers of high-risk AI systems to establish and document a post-market monitoring system proportionate to the nature of the AI and its risks. For healthcare, this means continuous monitoring of real-world performance -- including across demographic subgroups -- with a documented plan, not ad hoc dashboards.

## Penalties: Read the Tier Carefully

The penalty structure is tiered, and the tiers are commonly misquoted:

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
| --- | --- |
| Prohibited AI practices (Article 5) | €35M or 7% of global turnover |
| **High-risk AI obligations (most healthcare AI)** | **€15M or 3% of global turnover** |
| Supplying incorrect information to authorities | €7.5M or 1% of global turnover |

Most healthcare AI violations fall into the **€15M / 3%** tier, not the €35M / 7% tier. The higher tier applies only to prohibited practices under Article 5 -- social scoring, certain biometric categorization, manipulative systems. Confusing the two inflates the perceived risk and, worse, signals to regulators that your governance team does not know the statute.

## Why the Delay Makes Preparation Harder, Not Easier

Four structural factors mean the extra time is working capital, not slack:

1. **Notified Bodies are scarce.** As of Q1 2026, fewer than a dozen bodies have been designated to certify high-risk AI systems. Healthcare-specific competence is concentrated in a handful. Organizations waiting for the deadline will queue behind better-prepared competitors.

2. **Harmonized standards are still drafts.** JTC 21's work on AI risk management, data governance, and transparency is expected to finalize in 2026-2027. Early drafts raise the bar on subgroup evidence and lifecycle monitoring. Organizations that built compliance programs to the letter of the Act alone, without tracking the standards, will need to rework them.

3. **MDR/IVDR interaction is unsettled.** For AI embedded in medical devices, the question of how AI Act conformity assessment stacks on top of MDR/IVDR review remains open. Evidence generated now should be structured to satisfy both regimes.

4. **Plaintiffs and procurement are already moving.** US class-action litigation over healthcare AI denials (see the nH Predict discovery orders in *Estate of Lokken v. UnitedHealth*) is establishing de facto documentation expectations that transcend any single regulator's timeline. European hospital procurement is already asking vendors for subgroup performance data.

## What to Do With the Extra Time

Treat the delay as an 18-month window to build a **regulatory evidence package** that will survive both Notified Body review and adversarial scrutiny.

1. **Inventory every AI system, including embedded EHR features.** Most health systems underestimate their AI footprint by a factor of three or more.
2. **Map each system to Annex III and to MDR/IVDR classes.** Determine which regime -- or both -- applies.
3. **Produce subgroup performance reports now**, at the level of granularity draft JTC 21 standards indicate. Retrofitting this at the deadline is expensive.
4. **Document your post-market monitoring plan under Article 72.** Operating the plan for 12 months before certification is more persuasive than standing one up at deadline.
5. **Build the technical file as you go.** The Annex IV technical documentation requirements are extensive. Notified Bodies will expect evidence to be dated, versioned, and reproducible.
6. **Engage Notified Bodies early.** Capacity is the binding constraint.

The EU AI Act's substantive requirements have not been diluted. The deadline has moved because the ecosystem around the Act -- standards, Notified Bodies, Commission guidance -- is not yet capable of enforcing it. That ecosystem will be ready in 2027 and 2028. The organizations that use this window to build real evidence packages will pass first-time. The rest will discover that "we were waiting for clarity" is not a documented post-market monitoring system.
