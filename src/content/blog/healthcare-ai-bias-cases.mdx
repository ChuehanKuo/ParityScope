Healthcare AI bias is not a theoretical concern. It is a documented, measured, and in some cases litigated reality that has already affected hundreds of millions of patients. The cases below are not edge cases or hypotheticals. They are published findings from peer-reviewed research, and they reveal systemic patterns that every healthcare organization deploying AI should understand.

These six cases illustrate different mechanisms through which bias enters clinical AI systems, different patient populations that are harmed, and different lessons for organizations that want to do better.

## 1. The Optum Algorithm: Racial Bias in Care Management at Scale

**What happened:** In 2019, researchers at UC Berkeley published a landmark study in *Science* revealing that a widely used commercial algorithm -- developed by Optum and used by health systems across the United States -- exhibited significant racial bias in determining which patients should be referred to care management programs. The algorithm was used to guide care decisions for an estimated **200 million patients annually**.

**Who was affected:** Black patients were systematically less likely to be referred for additional care than equally sick white patients. At a given risk score threshold, Black patients were considerably sicker than white patients with the same score. The researchers estimated that remediating the bias would increase the percentage of Black patients receiving additional care from 17.7% to 46.5%.

**What the research showed:** The root cause was a design choice that seemed reasonable on its surface: the algorithm used healthcare spending as a proxy for healthcare need. Because Black patients in the United States historically incur lower healthcare costs -- not because they are healthier, but because they face systemic barriers to accessing care -- the algorithm systematically underestimated their medical needs. Obermeyer et al. (2019) demonstrated that at every level of actual health need, the algorithm assigned lower risk scores to Black patients.

**Lessons learned:** Proxy variables can encode and amplify existing structural inequities. An algorithm trained on biased data will reproduce those biases, even if race is never used as an explicit input variable. This case also demonstrated the scale at which a single biased algorithm can operate in healthcare, affecting care decisions for hundreds of millions of people before anyone detected the problem.

**Citation:** Obermeyer, Z., Powers, B., Vogeli, C., & Mullainathan, S. (2019). Dissecting racial bias in an algorithm used to manage the health of populations. *Science*, 366(6464), 447-453.

## 2. Dermatology AI: The Skin Tone Performance Gap

**What happened:** Multiple studies have documented that dermatology AI systems -- including deep learning models for skin cancer detection -- perform significantly worse on darker skin tones than on lighter skin tones. A 2021 study in *JAMA Dermatology* found that publicly available dermatology datasets contained a striking underrepresentation of images from patients with darker skin. A systematic review by Daneshjou et al. found that many AI dermatology tools showed accuracy drops of up to **29 percentage points** when evaluated on images of darker-skinned patients compared to lighter-skinned patients.

**Who was affected:** Patients with Fitzpatrick skin types IV through VI -- predominantly individuals of African, South Asian, Southeast Asian, and Hispanic descent -- face a higher risk of misdiagnosis or missed diagnosis when these systems are deployed. This is particularly concerning because melanoma in patients with darker skin is already diagnosed at later stages on average, contributing to worse outcomes.

**What the research showed:** The primary driver is data imbalance. Training datasets for dermatology AI are overwhelmingly composed of images from light-skinned patients. The widely used ISIC Archive, for example, has historically contained fewer than 5% of images from patients with dark skin. When models trained on these datasets encounter skin conditions presenting on darker skin -- where the visual characteristics can differ significantly -- they underperform. Daneshjou et al. (2022) specifically demonstrated that even state-of-the-art models failed to generalize across skin tones.

**Lessons learned:** AI systems are only as representative as their training data. When datasets reflect existing disparities in who receives care and whose conditions are documented, the resulting models perpetuate those disparities. Fairness in dermatology AI requires intentional data collection strategies, performance evaluation stratified by skin tone, and transparency about known limitations.

**Citations:** Daneshjou, R., Vodrahalli, K., Novoa, R. A., et al. (2022). Disparities in dermatology AI performance on a diverse, curated clinical image set. *Science Advances*, 8(32). Groh, M., Harris, C., Soenksen, L., et al. (2021). Evaluating deep neural networks trained on clinical images in dermatology with the Fitzpatrick 17k dataset. *CVPR Workshop*.

## 3. Epic's Sepsis Prediction Model: Widespread Deployment, Questionable Performance

**What happened:** Epic Systems, the largest electronic health record vendor in the United States, deployed a proprietary sepsis prediction model across hundreds of hospitals. In 2021, a study published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* by Wong et al. at the University of Michigan evaluated the model's real-world performance and found it **missed approximately 67% of sepsis cases** while generating a high volume of false alerts.

**Who was affected:** The model was deployed across a large proportion of US hospitals that use Epic's EHR platform, potentially affecting millions of patients. Missed sepsis cases can be fatal -- sepsis is a leading cause of in-hospital mortality, and delays in treatment significantly increase death rates.

**What the research showed:** The study found that Epic's sepsis model had an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) of 0.63 in real-world evaluation -- substantially lower than the 0.76 to 0.83 range Epic had reported. The model generated alerts for only 7% of patients who developed sepsis, while **18% of all alerts were false positives**. The researchers noted that the model's poor performance was partly attributable to differences between the development population and the populations where it was deployed, as well as the challenges of translating retrospective model performance to prospective clinical settings.

**Lessons learned:** Vendor-reported performance metrics may not translate to your patient population. Local validation is essential. Furthermore, this case highlights the danger of deploying clinical AI at scale without independent performance evaluation and the importance of post-deployment monitoring. A model with a 67% miss rate for a life-threatening condition is not just a statistical disappointment -- it is a patient safety concern.

**Citation:** Wong, A., Otles, E., Donnelly, J. P., et al. (2021). External validation of a widely implemented proprietary sepsis prediction model in hospitalized patients. *JAMA Internal Medicine*, 181(8), 1065-1070.

## 4. Pulse Oximetry: A Hardware Bias With Algorithmic Implications

**What happened:** A 2020 study published in *The New England Journal of Medicine* by Sjoding et al. demonstrated that pulse oximeters -- the ubiquitous clip-on devices that measure blood oxygen saturation -- systematically **overestimate oxygen levels in patients with darker skin pigmentation**. While this is a hardware bias rather than a purely algorithmic one, it has profound implications for any AI system that uses pulse oximetry data as an input.

**Who was affected:** Black patients were approximately **three times more likely** to have occult hypoxemia (dangerously low oxygen levels that the pulse oximeter failed to detect) compared to white patients. The study analyzed nearly 10,000 paired measurements and found that Black patients had undetected hypoxemia at nearly three times the rate.

**What the research showed:** Pulse oximeters estimate blood oxygen saturation by measuring the absorption of light through the finger. The calibration algorithms embedded in these devices were historically developed and validated primarily on lighter-skinned individuals. As a result, the devices produce systematically biased readings for patients with darker skin pigmentation. When these biased readings feed into downstream AI systems -- for example, models that predict respiratory deterioration or guide oxygen therapy -- the bias compounds.

**Lessons learned:** Bias can enter AI systems through upstream data sources, not just through the algorithm itself. Any clinical AI system that ingests pulse oximetry data inherits its racial bias unless corrective measures are applied. This case underscores why fairness auditing must extend beyond the model to encompass the entire data pipeline, including the measurement devices that generate input features.

**Citation:** Sjoding, M. W., Dickson, R. P., Iwashyna, T. J., Gay, S. E., & Valley, T. S. (2020). Racial bias in pulse oximetry measurement. *The New England Journal of Medicine*, 383(25), 2477-2478.

## 5. Chest X-Ray AI: Differential Performance Across Demographics

**What happened:** Multiple studies have shown that AI systems trained to interpret chest X-rays exhibit differential performance across racial, sex, and socioeconomic groups. A 2021 study by Seyyed-Kalantari et al. published in *Nature Medicine* found that **AI models consistently underdiagnosed conditions in underserved patient populations**, including Black patients, Hispanic patients, women, and patients covered by Medicaid.

**Who was affected:** The study analyzed three large chest X-ray datasets (MIMIC-CXR, CheXpert, and NIH Chest X-ray) and found that underdiagnosis rates were highest among intersectional subgroups -- for example, Black women with Medicaid coverage. These patients were most likely to have conditions present on their X-rays that the AI system failed to flag.

**What the research showed:** The disparities persisted even after controlling for disease prevalence and image quality. The researchers demonstrated that the AI models had learned to encode demographic information from the images themselves -- effectively learning correlations between patient demographics and diagnosis patterns that reflected historical care disparities rather than true disease characteristics. When the models performed worse for certain groups, it was not because those patients' conditions were harder to detect, but because the models had been trained on labels that reflected existing diagnostic inequities.

**Lessons learned:** Label bias -- where the ground truth labels used for training reflect historical patterns of underdiagnosis or overdiagnosis -- is a particularly insidious form of bias. Correcting it requires careful attention to how labels are generated, auditing for differential error rates across demographic groups, and potentially redefining what counts as ground truth.

**Citation:** Seyyed-Kalantari, L., Zhang, H., McDermott, M. B. A., Chen, I. Y., & Ghassemi, M. (2021). Underdiagnosis bias of artificial intelligence algorithms applied to chest radiographs in under-served patient populations. *Nature Medicine*, 27, 2176-2182.

## 6. Clinical NLP Bias: When Language Models Encode Stereotypes

**What happened:** Research has demonstrated that clinical natural language processing (NLP) models -- including large language models fine-tuned for medical applications -- can encode and reproduce racial and gender stereotypes present in clinical notes. A study by Zhang et al. (2020) showed that word embeddings trained on clinical text associated certain racial groups with negative clinical descriptors. Subsequent work, including Zack et al. (2024) published in *The Lancet Digital Health*, showed that large language models used for clinical decision support generated different diagnostic and treatment recommendations based on the patient's stated race, even when all other clinical details were identical.

**Who was affected:** Any patient whose care is influenced by NLP-powered systems is potentially affected. This includes patients whose records are processed by automated coding systems, clinical decision support tools that analyze free-text notes, or AI scribes and documentation assistants that generate clinical text.

**What the research showed:** Clinical notes contain well-documented biases in how clinicians describe patients of different races and genders. Terms like "non-compliant," "frequent flyer," or "drug-seeking" appear disproportionately in notes for Black patients and patients from lower socioeconomic backgrounds. When NLP models are trained on these notes, they learn to associate certain demographic groups with these descriptors. The result is that AI systems can amplify the subjective biases of individual clinicians into systematic patterns that affect entire populations.

**Lessons learned:** Training data sourced from clinical documentation inherits the biases of the clinicians who wrote it. Debiasing clinical NLP systems requires explicit attention to the language patterns in training data, evaluation of outputs for demographic disparities, and careful consideration of whether race and other demographic variables are being used appropriately versus reinforcing stereotypes.

**Citations:** Zhang, H., Lu, A. X., Abdalla, M., McDermott, M., & Ghassemi, M. (2020). Hurtful words: quantifying biases in clinical contextual word embeddings. *ACM Conference on Health, Inference, and Learning*. Zack, T., Lehman, E., Suzgun, M., et al. (2024). Assessing the potential of GPT-4 to perpetuate racial and gender biases in health care. *The Lancet Digital Health*, 6(1), e12-e22.

## The Common Thread

These six cases differ in their specifics, but they share common patterns:

- **Bias is often invisible to developers and deployers.** In each case, the bias was not identified until independent researchers evaluated the systems. Routine deployment processes failed to catch the problems.
- **Training data is the primary vector.** Whether through proxy variables, unrepresentative samples, biased labels, or contaminated text, the training data is where bias most commonly enters clinical AI systems.
- **Scale amplifies harm.** A biased clinical decision by an individual clinician affects one patient. A biased algorithm deployed across a health system affects thousands or millions.
- **Fairness auditing would have caught these issues.** Systematic evaluation of model performance across demographic subgroups -- the kind of analysis that regulations like the EU AI Act now require -- would have detected every one of these biases before or shortly after deployment.

The lesson for healthcare organizations is clear: **fairness auditing is not optional**. It is a clinical safety measure, a regulatory requirement, and an ethical obligation. Every AI system that influences patient care should be evaluated for demographic disparities before deployment and monitored continuously thereafter.

These cases are not reasons to abandon healthcare AI. They are reasons to govern it properly.
