In May 2024, Epic announced its **AI Trust and Assurance Software Suite**, a set of tools embedded in the EHR that lets health systems evaluate the AI models they run -- including a **Fairness Audit** module derived from the open-source toolkit developed jointly at the University of Chicago and Carnegie Mellon. Michigan Medicine has served as the reference customer, using the suite across predictive models running in Epic. Cerner/Oracle Health has signaled similar capabilities are coming to its stack. Meditech is unlikely to be far behind.

This is, on its face, a good development. Health systems should be able to see whether the models running inside their EHR perform consistently across patient populations. A built-in fairness dashboard lowers the operational cost of self-review.

It does not, however, replace independent verification.

The distinction matters because the question "did you test for bias" is rapidly becoming "can you prove it to someone who does not work for you." Notified Bodies under the EU AI Act, CHAI assurance labs, the Joint Commission's Responsible Use of Health Data standards, and -- increasingly -- plaintiff counsel in discovery will all ask variants of the same question. "We used the vendor's built-in tool" is a weak answer when the vendor has a commercial interest in the tool showing pass.

## The Structural Problem With Self-Audit

Every regulated industry that relies on trust has already run this experiment. Public companies do not audit their own financial statements. Airlines do not certify their own airworthiness. Drug manufacturers do not approve their own products. The reason is not that internal staff lack competence -- it is that independent verification is a structural requirement for the system to function.

Healthcare AI is now entering that category. The Epic Fairness Audit is useful for development and internal monitoring. But when the evidence is used to satisfy a regulatory obligation or defend against a discovery request, three problems surface.

### 1. Conflict of Interest

The vendor selling the AI model has a commercial incentive to show the model passes. This is not a claim about any specific engineer's integrity. It is a claim about the design of incentives. When Epic's Fairness Audit evaluates an Epic model against Epic-defined thresholds using Epic-curated cohort definitions, the unit of analysis is a closed loop.

Regulators are explicit about this. The EU AI Act's Article 43 conformity assessment requires, for certain high-risk systems, third-party involvement by a Notified Body precisely because self-assessment is treated as insufficient evidence. The FDA's January 2025 draft guidance on AI-enabled device software functions repeatedly emphasizes independent validation cohorts and change control evidence that is not generated by the same team building the model.

### 2. Limited Scope

Epic's Fairness Audit runs on models inside Epic. That leaves out:

- **Other EHR vendors.** Meditech, Cerner/Oracle Health, Athenahealth, and the long tail of specialty systems.
- **Third-party AI scribes.** Abridge, Nuance DAX, Suki, Ambience. These integrate with but are not fully contained in any one EHR.
- **Imaging AI.** Aidoc, Viz.ai, HeartFlow, dozens of FDA-cleared radiology tools that operate outside the EHR data model.
- **Pathology AI, genomics AI, and clinical research tools.**
- **Homegrown models.** Internally developed risk scores and data-science-team predictions that may or may not be wired into Epic's audit pipeline.

A large academic medical center typically runs AI from 20 or more distinct sources. An audit program that only covers one vendor's stack leaves the majority of the risk surface undocumented. The first time a Joint Commission surveyor or a plaintiff's expert asks "show me the audit evidence for every AI system in clinical use," an Epic-only answer is incomplete.

### 3. Regulatory and Litigation Defensibility

The defensibility test is not "did you run an audit." It is "can you produce reproducible, methodology-documented, independently-generated evidence."

In the *Estate of Lokken v. UnitedHealth* class action, the court's March 2026 order compelling UnitedHealth to disclose the nH Predict algorithm to plaintiffs marks a turning point. Discovery in healthcare AI cases is now reaching algorithm internals. When that subpoena lands, the hospital or plan that can produce:

- A third-party methodology document
- Input data hashes and reproducible pipelines
- Documented thresholds and decision rules set before the audit ran
- Version history of audits over time across all AI systems in use

...is in a fundamentally different posture than the one that can only produce screenshots from the vendor's built-in dashboard.

The Notified Body question under the EU AI Act is similar. CHAI's assurance lab network, which began operations in 2025, is explicit that assurance reports must be "generated by entities with documented independence from the model developer." Epic's Fairness Audit, run by Epic customers on Epic models, does not meet that criterion on its face.

## The Precedent: Why Big Four Exist

The comparison is not rhetorical. The modern financial audit profession emerged in the late 19th and early 20th century specifically because markets needed a third party whose reputation was staked on the accuracy of financial statements. The SEC's 1934 framework made this structural: public companies must hire an independent auditor, and that auditor cannot also be the company's consultant without firewalls.

Healthcare AI is following the same arc, compressed. The EU AI Act's Article 43 conformity assessment, the FDA's evolving expectations for predetermined change control plans, Section 1557's algorithmic non-discrimination provisions, and state-level AI laws in Colorado, California, and New York City all point in the same direction: the market will require evidence generated by parties independent of the model developer.

## What Independent Verification Actually Looks Like

Independent verification is not a branding exercise. It has specific technical properties that distinguish it from self-audit:

1. **Third-party methodology.** The audit framework, metric selection, and threshold definitions are published and maintained by a party that does not sell the model. The same methodology applies across vendors.
2. **Documented evidence package.** Inputs, configurations, random seeds, and intermediate outputs are captured and dated. Results are reproducible from the package.
3. **Input hashing and chain of custody.** The data used for the audit is fingerprinted so that reviewers can verify it was not silently swapped.
4. **Multi-vendor coverage.** The same audit methodology produces comparable evidence across Epic, Cerner/Oracle Health, Meditech, third-party scribes, imaging AI, and homegrown models. This allows a governance committee to compare apples to apples.
5. **Version history.** Audits over time are linked, so a regulator or plaintiff can see how disparities evolved and what the organization did about them.

Epic's Fairness Audit satisfies some of these properties within its scope. It does not, by construction, satisfy the independence or multi-vendor criteria.

## A Realistic Framing

None of this is an argument against using the vendor's tool. Epic's Fairness Audit is genuinely useful for:

- **Development-time self-checks** by model teams before a model ships
- **Operational monitoring** for customers already heavily invested in Epic
- **First-pass screening** to decide whether a deeper audit is needed
- **Day-to-day observability** for clinical governance committees

The line is about what evidence leaves the building. For the audit evidence that goes into your regulatory binder, your EU AI Act technical file, your Joint Commission survey folder, or your litigation discovery response, the source needs to be independent. That is not a technical limitation of the vendor's tool. It is a structural property of how regulated markets work.

The health systems that recognize this early -- and build an independent audit layer alongside, not instead of, their EHR vendor's tooling -- will spend the next two years generating evidence packages that are ready when the first subpoena or Notified Body review arrives. The rest will be building them under deadline pressure, with the vendor's dashboard and a very uncomfortable general counsel.
